# What is a Stock?

## The idea in one sentence
A stock is a small piece of ownership in a real company — when you buy one, you become a part-owner of that business.

## Why this matters
Everything in markets flows from this one idea. Before you can understand why prices move, why institutions behave the way they do, or what Delta One is detecting — you need to understand what you're actually buying when you buy a stock. Most beginners skip this and pay for it later.

## How it works
When a company wants to raise money, it can split itself into millions of tiny pieces called shares and sell those pieces to the public. This is called going public, or an IPO. Each share represents a fractional claim on the company's assets and future earnings.

If a company is worth $1,000,000 and it issues 1,000,000 shares, each share is worth $1. If the company grows and becomes worth $2,000,000, each share is now worth $2. That's the core mechanic — the share price reflects what the market collectively believes the company is worth right now.

The key word is *right now*. Stock prices don't reflect a company's past — they reflect what investors believe about its future. This is why a company can have record profits and still see its stock price fall, if those profits came in below what the market expected.

> **Key concept:** A stock price is not a report card on what a company has done. It's a live bet on what investors think it will do next.

## What this looks like in the market
NVIDIA (NVDA) was trading around $150 in early 2023. By mid-2024 it had crossed $1,000. The company's factories and employees didn't change overnight — what changed was the market's belief about future earnings from AI demand. The price moved on expectation, not current reality.

## Common mistake
Beginners treat a falling stock price as proof a company is failing. It isn't. A stock can fall because expectations were too high, because the broader market sold off, or because institutional players needed to raise cash — none of which has anything to do with the company's actual health.
