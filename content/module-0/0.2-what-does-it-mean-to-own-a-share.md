# What Does it Mean to Own a Share?

## The idea in one sentence
Owning a share means you hold a legal claim on a fraction of a company's value — its assets, its profits, and its future.

## Why this matters
Most retail traders never think about this. They treat stocks like casino chips — something to buy and sell for a price difference. Understanding what ownership actually means changes how you read price action, because you start to see that every price level is a negotiation between buyers who want ownership and sellers who want to exit it.

## How it works
When you own shares, you own a proportional slice of everything the company holds — its cash, its equipment, its intellectual property, its future revenue. If the company is liquidated tomorrow, shareholders get whatever is left after debts are paid.

In practice, most shareholders never think about liquidation. What they care about is two things: price appreciation (the stock goes up) and dividends (the company pays you a cut of its profits). Growth companies like NVDA reinvest profits instead of paying dividends, so shareholders there are betting almost entirely on price appreciation.

Ownership also comes with voting rights. Each share typically carries one vote on major company decisions — leadership, acquisitions, strategy. For retail traders holding 10 shares, this is largely symbolic. For institutions holding millions of shares, it's real power.

> **Key concept:** You're not just holding a price — you're holding a claim. That claim has real value independent of what someone else is willing to pay for it today.

## What this looks like in the market
When Meta (META) announced a $50 billion share buyback in 2023, the stock jumped. Why? Because buybacks reduce the total number of shares in circulation — which means each remaining share now represents a *larger* fraction of the same company. Ownership concentration went up without the company doing anything differently operationally.

## Common mistake
Beginners think of their position purely as a number on a screen. When the price drops, they panic because the number is smaller. Owners think differently — a lower price on the same claim means the same ownership is now cheaper to acquire. That shift in framing is the difference between reacting and reasoning.
