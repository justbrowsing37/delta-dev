# How Does a Stock Exchange Work?

## The idea in one sentence
A stock exchange is an organized marketplace where buyers and sellers submit orders, and a matching engine pairs them together at an agreed price.

## Why this matters
Price doesn't just happen — it's the output of a specific mechanical process. Once you understand how exchanges match orders, you understand why prices move in certain patterns, why liquidity matters, and what Delta One's strategy is actually exploiting.

## How it works
Every exchange runs an order book — a live list of all the buy orders (bids) and sell orders (asks) sitting unfilled. When you place a market order, you're agreeing to trade at whatever the best available price is right now. When you place a limit order, you're saying you'll only trade at a specific price — and your order sits in the book until someone meets it.

The gap between the best bid and the best ask is called the spread. On liquid stocks like NVDA or META, this spread is fractions of a cent. On thinly traded stocks, it can be dollars wide. That spread is the friction cost of every trade.

When a large order hits — say, an institution selling 500,000 shares — it doesn't just fill at one price. It eats through every bid in the book until it's fully filled. Each level it consumes pushes the price lower. This is called market impact, and it's one of the core reasons big players move differently from retail traders.

> **Key concept:** Price is not set by any single person or entity — it's the output of millions of competing orders, cleared by a matching engine in microseconds.

## What this looks like in the market
The NYSE and NASDAQ handle billions of shares daily. NVDA alone trades over 40 million shares on an average day. That volume means the order book is deep — large trades can be absorbed without moving the price dramatically. This depth is exactly what makes NVDA a viable symbol for sweep-style strategies: there's enough institutional participation to create clean, readable liquidity levels.

## Common mistake
Beginners assume the price they see on a chart is *the* price of the stock. It's actually the price of the *last* trade. The real price — what you'd actually get if you traded right now — depends entirely on the current state of the order book, which changes every millisecond.
