# Bulls, Bears, and Market Sentiment

## The idea in one sentence
Market sentiment is the collective emotional state of all participants — and it's one of the most powerful short-term drivers of price, often overriding fundamentals entirely.

## Why this matters
Institutions run quantitative models. Retail traders run on emotion. Understanding sentiment lets you anticipate how the crowd will behave at key price levels — which is exactly the knowledge that sweep strategies exploit. The crowd's predictable fear and greed create the liquidity clusters that Delta One's engine is built to trade.

## How it works
A bull market is one defined by rising prices and broadly positive sentiment — investors are willing to take risk, buy dips, and hold positions. A bear market is the opposite: falling prices, rising fear, and a tendency to sell strength and exit positions.

But sentiment isn't just a binary bull/bear switch. It exists on a spectrum, and it shifts across different timeframes simultaneously. A stock can be in a long-term bull trend (weekly chart), a short-term bearish correction (daily chart), and a micro-level oversold bounce (hourly chart) — all at once.

Sentiment is visible in price action. When price approaches a known support level and bounces sharply, that's bullish sentiment in action — buyers stepped in aggressively. When price breaks below a known support level and sellers pile on, that's bearish sentiment cascading through stop losses.

Fear moves faster than greed. Sell-offs are typically sharper and faster than rallies. This is because loss aversion — the psychological pain of losing money — triggers faster, more panicked responses than the pleasure of gaining it.

> **Key concept:** Sentiment creates predictable behavior at predictable price levels. Bulls cluster their stop losses below support. Bears cluster theirs above resistance. These clusters are the liquidity that institutional traders — and Delta One — are built to find.

## What this looks like in the market
In early 2024, NVDA was in an extreme bullish sentiment environment driven by AI hype. Every dip was bought aggressively. Retail sentiment was uniformly bullish — which meant stop losses were stacked in predictable places below every recent swing low. This kind of sentiment uniformity is exactly when sweep setups become high-probability: the crowd is positioned the same way, so their exits are in the same place.

## Common mistake
Beginners try to trade against sentiment too early — shorting a stock because it "looks overbought" or buying one because it "looks oversold." Sentiment can remain extreme far longer than your account can remain solvent. The edge isn't in fighting sentiment — it's in understanding where sentiment-driven positioning creates exploitable liquidity clusters.
