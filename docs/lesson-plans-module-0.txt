LESSON 0.1 — "What is a stock?"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Learning Objective:
    Understand that a stock represents a fractional ownership stake in a
    company and that price reflects the market's current opinion of value.

  Hook:
    "Every time you buy a coffee, someone somewhere owns a piece of that
    company — this is what that actually means."

  Interaction Type:  reveal

  interaction_data:
    candles: [50 synthetic OHLCV points, base ~$100, uptrend to ~$148,
              pullback days 28-32, consolidation days 38-42]
    symbol: "AAPL"
    timeframe: "1D"
    narration: [
      { candle_index: 5,
        text: "Each one of these candles represents a single day of trading.
               The body shows the open and close price; the wicks show the
               highest and lowest price reached." },
      { candle_index: 15,
        text: "Notice how the price is generally rising. That means more people
               want to buy than sell — demand exceeds supply, so the price
               moves up." },
      { candle_index: 30,
        text: "Here we see a pullback — a short dip against the trend.
               Pullbacks are normal. Some traders take profits, and new buyers
               step in at the lower price." },
      { candle_index: 45,
        text: "See how the price action connects to real events? Company news,
               earnings, and market sentiment all shape this chart. Price is
               just the market's collective opinion in motion." },
    ]

  Chart Type: Candlestick (bullish #f59e0b, bearish #ef4444, bg #1e222d)

  Animation: Candles animate in left-to-right at ~40ms each (~2s total).
             Narration cards appear at bottom of chart area on designated
             candles, pausing the animation for 2.5s.

  Key Takeaway:
    "A stock is ownership. Price is just the market's current opinion of what
    that ownership is worth — and opinions change every second."

  Reflect Questions:
    Q1: "What does it mean when a stock price goes up?"
        Correct answer: "more buyers than sellers"
        Acceptable: ["demand exceeds supply", "more buying than selling",
                     "more people want to buy", "buyers outnumber sellers"]
        Explanation: "When the price goes up, it means more people want to buy
                     the stock than sell it at the current price. The buyers
                     are winning the auction."

    Q2: "What does owning one share of a company give you?"
        Correct answer: "a small ownership stake"
        Acceptable: ["ownership", "a piece of the company",
                     "ownership in the company", "a share of ownership",
                     "equity", "partial ownership", "a stake in the company"]
        Explanation: "A single share represents a tiny slice of ownership in
                     that company. You own a fraction of its assets and future
                     profits."

  Notes Content:
    title:   "What is a Stock?"
    bullets: ["Ownership stake", "Shareholders", "Price = opinion"]
    questions: ["Why do prices move?", "What does one share represent?"]

  Visual Description:
    [← Exit Lesson]
    AAPL · 1D · Lesson 0.1
    [Candlestick chart area — animated reveal]
    [Narration card at bottom of chart — styled box with left accent border]
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────
    Hook text (large, centered)
    Key Takeaway (accent-themed block)
    Reflection Question 1: [> input] [Submit]
    (after correct) Reflection Question 2: [> input] [Submit]
    (after both correct) [Mark Complete]

LESSON 0.2 — "What does it mean to own a share?"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Learning Objective:
    Understand the rights and risks that come with being a shareholder,
    including voting rights, dividends, and the possibility of total loss.

  Hook:
    "You bought one share. Now what do you actually get? A vote, a slice of
    profits, and a front-row seat to the company's future."

  Interaction Type:  reveal

  interaction_data:
    type: "ownership-breakdown"
    components: [
      {
        label: "Voting Rights",
        description: "One share = one vote at shareholder meetings on major
                      company decisions like board elections and mergers.",
        icon: "vote"
      },
      {
        label: "Dividends",
        description: "If the company distributes profits, you get a portion
                      proportional to your shares. Not all companies pay
                      dividends.",
        icon: "cash"
      },
      {
        label: "Capital Appreciation",
        description: "If the company grows, each share becomes worth more.
                      You can sell later for a profit.",
        icon: "trend-up"
      },
      {
        label: "Risk of Loss",
        description: "If the company fails, your shares can become worthless.
                      You are not liable for the company's debts — but your
                      investment can go to zero.",
        icon: "warning"
      }
    ]
    animation: "staggered-reveal" — each component card fades in one at a
               time with a 1s interval, starting from the top.

  Key Takeaway:
    "Ownership = upside potential + downside risk. You can lose your entire
    investment if the company fails."

  Reflect Questions:
    Q1: "What is one right that owning a share gives you?"
        Correct answer: "voting rights"
        Acceptable: ["vote", "voting", "a vote at shareholder meetings",
                     "vote at shareholder meetings", "voting at shareholder meetings",
                     "a say in company decisions"]
        Explanation: "Each share gives you one vote at shareholder meetings.
                     You can vote on major decisions like who sits on the
                     board of directors."

    Q2: "If a company goes bankrupt, what happens to your shares?"
        Correct answer: "they become worthless"
        Acceptable: ["worthless", "you lose your investment", "you lose your money",
                     "you lose everything", "value goes to zero", "zero",
                     "they are worth nothing"]
        Explanation: "If a company fails, its stock can become worthless.
                     Shareholders are last in line for any remaining value —
                     after lenders and bondholders are paid. Your investment
                     can go to zero."

  Notes Content:
    title:   "What does it mean to own a share?"
    bullets: ["Voting rights", "Dividends", "Capital appreciation", "Risk of loss"]
    questions: ["What rights do shareholders have?", "What happens if the company fails?"]

  Visual Description:

LESSON 0.3 — "How does a stock exchange work?"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Learning Objective:
    Understand the role of a stock exchange as a central matching point where
    buyers and sellers meet, and what happens when you place an order.

  Hook:
    "You click 'buy.' In 0.003 seconds, your order travels across the
    internet, meets a matching engine, and finds someone on the other side
    willing to sell. This is how."

  Interaction Type:  simulation

  interaction_data:
    type: "order-flow"
    steps: [
      {
        label: "1. You place an order",
        description: "You tell your broker: 'Buy 10 shares of Apple at the
                      current market price.'",
        role: "you",
        animation: "user-input"
      },
      {
        label: "2. Your broker routes it",
        description: "Your broker sends the order to the exchange (NYSE,
                      Nasdaq, etc.) where Apple is listed.",
        role: "broker",
        animation: "arrow-right"
      },
      {
        label: "3. The matching engine receives it",
        description: "The exchange's electronic matching engine receives your
                      order and looks for a matching sell order.",
        role: "exchange",
        animation: "pulse"
      },
      {
        label: "4. A match is found",
        description: "Another investor is selling 10 shares at $150. The
                      engine pairs you. Trade executed.",
        role: "match",
        animation: "flash"
      },
      {
        label: "5. Confirmation",
        description: "You now own 10 shares. The seller has $1,500. The
                      exchange updates the last traded price to $150.",
        role: "done",
        animation: "checkmark"
      }
    ]
    auto_advance_ms: 1500  — each step auto-advances after 1.5s with a
                             directional arrow animation flowing left to right

  Key Takeaway:
    "An exchange is a central matching point. Buyers meet sellers, and the
    current price is whatever the last trade happened at."

  Reflect Questions:
    Q1: "What does a stock exchange do?"
        Correct answer: "matches buyers and sellers"
        Acceptable: ["matches buyers with sellers", "matches buy and sell orders",
                     "matches orders", "pairs buyers and sellers",
                     "connects buyers and sellers"]
        Explanation: "A stock exchange is a central marketplace that matches
                     people who want to buy shares with people who want to
                     sell them. The exchange doesn't own the shares — it just
                     facilitates the trade."

    Q2: "Where does your buy order go after you submit it?"
        Correct answer: "to the exchange"
        Acceptable: ["to the stock exchange", "the exchange",
                     "exchange matching engine", "matching engine"]
        Explanation: "Your order goes from your broker to the exchange's
                     matching engine, which finds a matching sell order. The
                     whole process takes milliseconds."

  Notes Content:
    title:   "How does a stock exchange work?"
    bullets: ["Buyers meet sellers", "Matching engines", "Millisecond execution"]
    questions: ["What happens when you place an order?",
                "Where do orders get matched?"]

  Visual Description:
    [← Exit Lesson]
    Order Flow Simulation
    ┌──────┐    ┌────────┐    ┌──────────┐    ┌──────┐    ┌────────────┐
    │  You ├───►│ Broker ├───►│ Exchange ├───►│ Match│───►│ Confirmed  │
    │ buy  │    │ routes │    │ receives │    │ found│    │ You own 10 │
    └──────┘    └────────┘    └──────────┘    └──────┘    └────────────┘
       ↑ Each node lights up sequentially with an arrow animation flowing
         left to right. Each step shows its description text below.
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────
    Hook text

LESSON 0.4 — "What moves a stock price?"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Learning Objective:
    Understand that stock prices move due to shifts in supply and demand
    driven by earnings, news, sentiment, and algorithmic trading.

  Hook:
    "A stock price doesn't move because it 'feels like going up.' It moves
    because someone just bought, and someone else just sold — and they
    disagreed on what it was worth."

  Interaction Type:  reveal

  interaction_data:
    type: "news-chart"
    candles: [40 synthetic OHLCV points, base $100, with distinct price
              reactions to simulated news events]
    symbol: "AAPL"
    timeframe: "1D"
    events: [
      { candle_index: 8,
        type: "positive",
        headline: "Apple reports record quarterly earnings — profit up 22%",
        effect: "Three green candles, price jumps +4%",
        color: "#69b85d" },
      { candle_index: 18,
        type: "negative",
        headline: "iPhone sales miss analyst estimates",
        effect: "Two red candles, price drops -3%",
        color: "#da6b8c" },
      { candle_index: 28,
        type: "neutral",
        headline: "Fed holds interest rates steady — markets take a breath",
        effect: "Sideways consolidation for 4 days",
        color: "#5c6672" },
      { candle_index: 35,
        type: "positive",
        headline: "Apple announces new AI features at developer conference",
        effect: "Three green candles, price rallies +5%",
        color: "#69b85d" }
    ]
    annotation: "Event markers appear as colored dots above the candle with
                 a small headline label. The chart pauses briefly at each
                 event to display the headline and explanation."

  Narration (same pattern as 0.1, but tied to events instead):
    narration: [
      { candle_index: 8,
        text: "When a company reports strong earnings, more investors want to
               buy. Demand goes up, supply stays the same — price rises." },
      { candle_index: 18,
        text: "Bad news means fewer people want to own the stock. Sellers
               outnumber buyers, and the price drops." },
      { candle_index: 28,
        text: "Sometimes nothing dramatic happens. Price drifts sideways as
               buyers and sellers wait for the next catalyst." },
      { candle_index: 35,
        text: "New product announcements can reignite demand. The market
               prices in expected future growth." },
    ]

  Key Takeaway:
    "Price is a real-time auction. Every tick represents a transaction between
    a buyer and a seller who disagree on value."

  Reflect Questions:
    Q1: "What causes a stock's price to go up?"
        Correct answer: "more buyers than sellers"
        Acceptable: ["demand exceeds supply", "more buying than selling",
                     "high demand", "increased demand", "buyers outnumber sellers",
                     "more demand than supply"]
        Explanation: "When more people want to buy a stock than sell it, the
                     price rises. It is basic supply and demand — the same
                     force that sets the price of anything scarce."

    Q2: "Can company news affect a stock price even if the company itself
         hasn't changed?"
        Correct answer: "yes"
        Acceptable: ["yes", "yes it can", "yes news affects sentiment",
                     "yes because expectations change"]
        Explanation: "Yes. The stock price reflects what investors believe
                     the company will be worth in the future. News changes
                     those expectations, which changes the price — even if
                     the company's actual value hasn't changed yet."

  Notes Content:
    title:   "What moves a stock price?"
    bullets: ["Supply and demand", "Earnings drive moves", "News changes sentiment"]
    questions: ["Why does price go up and down?",
                "Can news move the price?"]

  Visual Description:
    [← Exit Lesson]
    AAPL · 1D · Lesson 0.4
    [Candlestick chart with colored event markers above the candles]
    [At each event marker, a headline card animates in with the news text
     and a colored left border matching the event type]
    [Narration card at bottom of chart area matching the event]

LESSON 0.5 — "Bulls, bears, and market sentiment"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Learning Objective:
    Understand the concepts of bullish (expecting price to rise) and bearish
    (expecting price to fall) sentiment, and how the tug-of-war between the
    two drives market movements.

  Hook:
    "Every market move is a fight between two tribes: the bulls who think
    price will go up, and the bears who think it will go down. Whoever has
    more momentum wins the round."

  Interaction Type:  classify

  interaction_data:
    type: "bull-vs-bear"
    rounds: [
      {
        scenario: "A company announces higher-than-expected profits.",
        correct: "bullish",
        explanation: "Strong profits mean the company is doing well. Bulls
                     expect the stock to rise."
      },
      {
        scenario: "A major competitor releases a better product.",
        correct: "bearish",
        explanation: "A competitor winning hurts the company's future sales.
                     Bears expect the stock to fall."
      },
      {
        scenario: "The company's CEO buys $5 million worth of shares.",
        correct: "bullish",
        explanation: "When insiders buy their own stock, it signals confidence
                     that the stock is undervalued. Bulls see this as a
                     positive sign."
      },
      {
        scenario: "Interest rates rise, making borrowing more expensive.",
        correct: "bearish",
        explanation: "Higher rates mean companies pay more to borrow money,
                     which can reduce profits. Bears expect the stock to fall."
      },
      {
        scenario: "The stock price has fallen 30% in a month with no bad news.",
        correct: "bullish",
        explanation: "A sharp drop without bad news can mean the sell-off is
                     overdone. Value-seeking bulls step in to buy the dip."
      }
    ]
    presentation: "Each scenario is shown as a card. The user taps either
                   'Bullish' or 'Bearish'. Correct answers advance. Wrong
                   answers show the explanation and let the user try again.
                   Progress is shown as a bar: 'Scenario 3/5'."

  Key Takeaway:
    "Markets are driven by the tug-of-war between bulls and bears. Sentiment
    tells you who is in control."

  Reflect Questions:
    Q1: "What does it mean to be 'bullish' on a stock?"
        Correct answer: "expect the price to rise"
        Acceptable: ["think the price will go up", "believe the price will rise",
                     "expect the stock to go up", "think it will go up",
                     "price will rise", "expect price to increase"]
        Explanation: "Being bullish means you expect the price to go up.
                     Bulls buy stocks hoping to sell them later for a higher
                     price."

    Q2: "In a tug-of-war between bulls and bears, what happens when bearish
         sentiment takes over?"
        Correct answer: "price falls"
        Acceptable: ["the price falls", "price goes down", "prices drop",
                     "the market goes down", "sellers take over",
                     "more selling than buying"]
        Explanation: "When bearish sentiment dominates, more people want to
                     sell than buy. The price falls as sellers compete to find
                     buyers."

  Notes Content:
    title:   "Bulls, bears, and market sentiment"
    bullets: ["Bulls expect price to rise", "Bears expect price to fall",
              "Sentiment = who is in control"]
    questions: ["What is bullish sentiment?",
                "What happens when bears take over?"]

  Visual Description:
    [← Exit Lesson]
